Heliotechnology Solar Energy 1954–1988

Heliotechnology & Solar Power

How a nuclear physicist built the institutional and scientific foundations of solar energy in Central Asia

In the 1960s, Giyas Yakubovich Umarov made a strategic pivot that would define his lasting legacy. He shifted his focus from fundamental nuclear research to heliotechnology—the applied science of converting solar radiation into usable energy. This was not a retreat from rigor; it was its application to the most abundant energy source on Earth, in one of the sunniest regions of the world.

The Tower-Type Solar Plant (1954)

As early as 1954, Umarov proposed a tower-type solar power plant featuring a heliostat field—an array of mirrors directing sunlight to a central receiver atop a tower. This concept predated the modern Concentrated Solar Power (CSP) industry by decades. The design principle Umarov articulated became the basis for installations such as:

  • The STP facility in Gazli, Uzbekistan
  • Spain's PS10 and PS20 solar towers (operational 2007–2009)
  • The Ivanpah Solar Electric Generating System in California (2014)

Umarov's early proposal demonstrated that the fundamental engineering logic of tower-type CSP was already well-understood in the Soviet context, decades before the global energy crisis of the 1970s made solar power a Western priority.

Founding Heliotechnika / Applied Solar Energy

Umarov founded and served as deputy chief editor of Heliotechnika, which was published internationally by Allerton Press (later Springer) as Applied Solar Energy. This journal became the premier Soviet publication on solar energy and remains in publication today, serving as a bridge between the Russian-language and English-language scientific communities.

The founding of Heliotechnika was not merely an editorial achievement—it created an institutional anchor for over 300 researchers in Uzbekistan and established solar energy as a recognized scientific discipline within the Soviet Academy of Sciences.

The Large Solar Furnace

Umarov was the primary champion behind the creation of the Large Solar Furnace near Tashkent, a high-concentration solar research facility completed in 1987. This installation was used for sophisticated materials testing and thermal processing under extreme conditions, including:

  • Synthesis and testing of new refractory materials
  • Study of material behavior under temperatures exceeding 3,000°C
  • Research into solar-driven chemical processes

The facility represented the culmination of Umarov's vision: a world-class research instrument that proved solar energy could achieve the temperatures and precision required for advanced materials science and industrial processes.

Concentrating Systems: Theory and Practice

In 1977, Umarov co-authored Theory and Calculation of Heliotechnical Concentrating Systems with R.A. Zakhidov and A.A. Vainer. This monograph provided the definitive mathematical treatment of:

  • Optical characteristics of parabolic, paraboloidal, and flat mirror concentrators
  • Flux distribution modeling at the focal point
  • Thermal efficiency calculations for various receiver geometries
  • Lightweight thin light-focusing mirrors (investigated further in a 1984 paper)

Solar Ponds and Thermal Collectors

Umarov's 1971 paper "The optical characteristics of a solar pond" examined the physics of solar ponds—bodies of salt water that trap solar heat in their lower layers. This research contributed to understanding how natural and artificial water bodies could serve as both solar collectors and thermal storage media, complementing his aquifer thermal storage work.

Thin-Film Photovoltaics

A 1977 paper, "Effect of Base Doping on the Electrical and Photo-electric Properties of the Thin-Film Cu2S-CdS Heterojunction," demonstrated Umarov's engagement with photovoltaic technology. The Cu2S-CdS thin-film heterojunction was one of the earliest thin-film solar cell architectures, and this research explored how doping profiles affected device performance—work that anticipated the modern thin-film PV industry.

Institutional Impact

Beyond his personal research output, Umarov's institutional contributions were transformative:

  • 300+ researchers trained and supported through the heliotechnology program
  • 54 doctoral dissertations supervised across nuclear physics and solar energy
  • 6 popular science books that brought solar energy concepts to the general public
  • Establishment of Tashkent as a recognized international center for high-concentration solar research
